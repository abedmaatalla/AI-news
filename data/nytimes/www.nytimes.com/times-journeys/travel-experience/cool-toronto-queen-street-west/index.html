Cool Toronto: Queen Street West

  • Arts & Culture
  • City Tours

Itinerary inspired by 36 Hours in Toronto.

Even with 2.8 million people, Toronto still feels like a small city, and Queen Street West represents both the new, happening Toronto and some of its old values. In a route developed by Urban Adventures and The New York Times, get a taste of this diverse city’s live-and-let-live ethos and vibrant street culture, and understand the challenges it faces as developers and big brands look to capitalize on its vibe.

New York Times Exclusives

  • Meet the chef Guy Rawlings in his restaurant kitchen and discuss Canadian ingredients and cuisine.
  • Participate in a street-art project in Graffiti Alley using chalk spray.
  • Meet and interact with the owners or staff members at small businesses, including Toronto’s oldest skate and snowboard shop, a Toronto-themed store, a legendary music venue and a local dive bar.

Highlights

  • Hear different perspectives on the neighborhood and its history from the people who live and work in the area.
  • Learn how business improvement areas, which had their start in Toronto in 1970, are working to help local businesses survive as taxes and rents go up and more people are shopping online.
  • Understand the etiquette of the graffiti subculture and learn how to read the writing on the walls.
  • Eat and drink like a local.

Itinerary

Hour 1: Canada’s MTV and Skateboard Culture

Meet near the Art Gallery of Ontario and walk past the Ontario College of Art and Design to Queen Street West. As you walk, hear from your guide how Queen Street West became the epicenter of youth culture and trends in music, art and fashion. Part of its history stems from the opening of MuchMusic, Canada’s MTV, in the early 80s. That made its headquarters and the surrounding neighborhood a draw for young people who wanted to see their favorite bands and interact with them in the open-concept street level. Stop at So Hip It Hurts, the oldest skateboard and snowboard shop in Toronto. Here you’ll see the celebrity wall filled with photos of many of the actors and musicians who shopped here, like Adam Sandler, Jack Johnson, the Beastie Boys and Robin Williams. The store has seen a lot in its 25 years in business, and you can talk to the staff about the challenge to stay relevant as trends change and the neighborhood gentrifies around them. Even if you don’t skate or snowboard, this is a don’t-miss spot.

Hour 2: Unique Toronto

Visit the 401 Richmond Building, a former industrial space turned into art galleries. Inside you’ll find the Spacing Store, Toronto’s unofficial gift shop, filled with goods made in the city or that feature local imagery and inside jokes. (Ask your guide what a “trash panda” is.) The store arose out of a publication called Spacing, created in 2003 to cover issues like urban design, sustainable development and public art and transit, which the founders felt were not covered sufficiently by the local media. Talk with the staff and learn how this building was recently saved from closing after the taxes on its land went up. Then stop for a beer at the Legendary Horseshoe Tavern and see the stage that since 1947 has hosted acts like Willie Nelson, Loretta Lynn, Conway Twitty (it started as a country bar), the Rolling Stones, the Ramones, Nickelback, the Strokes, the Police and Barenaked Ladies over the decades. The bartenders can tell you stories.

Hour 3: Graffiti and a Park

Walk the laneway known as Graffiti Alley to see some of the most varied street art in the city and maybe spot an artist at work. The works can be elegant, abstract, eerie or even fantastical, but there’s no denying this is art. Elsewhere graffiti inspires controversy, but in this area it’s become part of the landscape. Grab some nontoxic chalk spray and leave your own (temporary) mark in the alley. Don’t forget the #selfies. Then check out the local shops in the trendy stretch of Queen Street managed by the West Queen West Business Improvement Area. The world’s first B.I.A. was founded in Toronto in 1970, and these public-private partnership models have expanded to many urban areas. Learn how the local B.I.A. has helped this neighborhood become one of the most successful and popular places to live, work and shop. If the weather is good, walk through part of Trinity Bellwoods Park, where locals flock during the warmer months for picnics and the discreet drinking of adult beverages from covert thermoses or paper bag-wrapped cans and bottles. Keep an eye out for the legendary white squirrels: Spotting them is supposed to be good luck.

Hour 4: Canadian Food and Drink

Stop by Montgomery House Restaurant to chat with the chef, Guy Montgomery, about what constitutes Canadian cuisine and to taste some house-made specialties. Perhaps it will be sourdough bread or something in season. Then walk up to Ossington Avenue, considered to be the next trendy neighborhood, with vintage shops, ice cream parlors and restaurants galore, as well as more graffiti-filled alleys. Finally, relax at Sweaty Betty’s. Don’t let the tattoos or piercings of some of the patrons scare you off: Though this is a local dive bar, it’s a welcoming one. The actress and director Drew Barrymore chose to come here after she ditched her own opening party for the movie “Whip It” at the Toronto International Film Festival. It’s small, dark and cozy, with a great patio in back. Have one of the off-menu drinks that you have to know about to order.

After the tour you may want to check out some places for dinner, including the new restaurants that have popped up on Ossington Avenue, or neighborhood favorites like the Golden Turtle. Another possibility is the historic Lakeview Restaurant, whose 1950s retro style has been in many movies and TV shows, including the 2017 Academy Award-winning film “The Shape of Water.”

 

Tour Inclusions: Snack of house-made seasonal specialties like sourdough bread and pickles, small beer at The Horseshoe Tavern, chalk spray and gloves provided, off-menu drink specialty at Sweaty Betty’s.

Tour Exclusions: Food and drink not listed in the itinerary, gratuity for your guide.

Children: No children allowed. Travelers under 19 years of age are not permitted to join this tour.
