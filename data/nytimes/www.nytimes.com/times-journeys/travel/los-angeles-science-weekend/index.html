L.A. Science Weekend: Natural History and Space

  • Science & Nature
  • Weekends
Itinerary
  • 1 The Night Sky
  • 2 The Natural World of Animals or Space
  • 3 The Unknown Universe

Spend three days immersed in scientific learning in Los Angeles, focused on natural history and zoology or space and aviation. This two-track program from The New York Times and Atlas Obscura includes panels, exclusive visits and access to scientists and venues to get up close to everything from telescopes and taxidermy to dinosaur skeletons and space artifacts.

Accompanied by New York Times journalists and scientific experts, meet lab specialists, particle physicists, science illustrators, dinosaur researchers and urban astronomers. Start with a specially curated display from the Moore Lab of Zoology, followed by a rooftop workshop on urban astronomy. Panels of reporters and experts will walk you through the current challenges and triumphs of their fields. Break into smaller groups and choose from several visits and focused discussions as you explore natural history and zoology or space and aviation. Visit the Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County after hours for a happy hour with special visits to the Dino Lab in the company of a top paleontology expert.

Scheduled Experts

  • Kenneth Chang

    May 10–12 departure
    Kenneth Chang is a Science reporter for The New York Times. With a background in physics, he specializes in writing about NASA and the physical sciences. His articles have included the mysteries of ghostly elementary particles known as neutrinos, the discovery of a planet with the puffiness of cork around a distant star and how the American Museum of Natural History in New York demoted Pluto years before the rest of the world.

  • Cornelia Dean

    May 10–12 departure
    Cornelia Dean is a science writer and former science editor of The New York Times and a distinguished visiting lecturer at Brown University. As science editor of The Times, she was responsible for coverage of science, engineering, health and medical news. In her editing tenure of the science section members of its staff have won the Pulitzer Prize twice and were finalists three times.

  • John Schwartz

    May 10–12 departure
    John Schwartz is a reporter at The Times who writes about climate change. In his career at The Times, he has also covered the space program, a beat that took him to a half dozen shuttle launches and two zero-gravity flights, and to Space City in Russia, where cosmonauts and astronauts train together to fly to the International Space Station.

  • Anahad O’Connor

    May 10–12 departure
    Anahad O’Connor, who joined The Times in 2003, covers consumer health, medicine, science and other topics. A graduate of Yale with a degree in psychology and a focus on neuroscience and child studies, he is the author of four books, including the best-selling “Never Shower in a Thunderstorm.”

Journey Highlights

  • Enjoy a special after-hours event at the Natural History Museum focusing on the Dinosaur Institute, with many interactive exhibits and small-group tours.
  • Attend a workshop on deep sky objects, astronomy apps, astrophotography and image-stacking software, and learn to take better sky pictures on your smartphone.
  • From the private Dino Lab at the Natural History Museum of Los Angeles to a visit to the Carnegie Observatories, get special access to some of the city’s most fascinating sites for natural history, zoology, space and aviation.

Day 1

The Night Sky

Kick things off with a private reception at the LINE Hotel in Downtown Los Angeles, with beautiful views of the city skyline. Have a round of welcome drinks, get to know some of your fellow travelers, and be sure to take a closer look at the bird specimens on display, courtesy of the Moore Lab of Zoology.

Attend a brief orientation and introduction to your New York Times journalists, followed by dinner with them. Then meet the directors of SkiesAway, which has been hosting star parties around the world for three decades, for an introduction to urban astronomy. Follow them to a nearby city rooftop for a workshop on deep sky objects, astronomy apps, astrophotography, image-stacking software and flyovers of the International Space Station. In addition to learning how to use a proper telescope, you’ll pick up tricks for capturing stunning nighttime images on your smartphone.

Day 2

The Natural World of Animals or Space

After breakfast on your own, choose one of two morning panels to attend, both featuring New York Times journalists discussing the relationship between science and the press. For those focusing on natural history and zoology, the journalists will discuss how different global regions report on the natural world. If you’re focusing on space and aviation, join journalists discussing the future of space journalism. Both panels will include time for Q&A.

• Panel A: How different global regions report on the natural world.
• Panel B: The future of space journalism.

After lunch on your own, join one of six excursions, each with a different topic and destination. (You can choose a second topic for tomorrow; most are offered both days.)

Your choices for the natural history and zoology track are:
• Taxidermy for Research and Display
• The Art of Illustrating Science
• History, Ethics and the Economics of Zoos

Your choices for the space and aviation track are these:
• Telescopes, Astronomy and Machines
• Launching Apollo
• The History and Future of Humans in Flight

After your session, take some time to relax. Then, attend a private, after-hours soiree at the Natural History Museum, home to over 35 million specimens, some as old as 4.5 billion years. Begin with the display of touchable dinosaur casts, followed by welcome remarks in the “Dueling Dinos” Grand Foyer. Get an overview of the Dinosaur Institute, which has been collecting specimens for more than a century, and break into smaller groups to tour the private Dino Lab, where you can learn about conservation practices and see the fossils. Two other galleries will be open for self-guided exploration.

Dinner and the rest of the night is on your own; you will get a list of recommendations for nearby restaurants and nightlife.

Day 3

The Unknown Universe

After breakfast on your own, attend another workshop of your choice. Your choices for the natural history and zoology track are these:
• Taxidermy for Research and Display
• The Art of Illustrating Science
• History, Ethics and the Economics of Zoos

Your choices for the space and aviation track are these:
• Launching Apollo
• The History and Future of Humans in Flight
• Breakthroughs in Design for Space Exploration

Have lunch on your own and return to the LINE Hotel for a final send-off from the people who bring you the popular podcast “Daniel and Jorge Explain the Universe.” Daniel Whiteson, a particle physicist who conducts research at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN, and Jorge Cham, an engineer turned online cartoonist with a Ph.D. in robotics, combine science storytelling and live cartooning to remind you just how little humans still know about the universe.

The program officially concludes at 3:30 p.m. You can catch flights (or cabs) home, extend your stay, or abandon everything and change your career path to one of the scientific fields you encountered in your three days. Ask for help with any of those options.

The Science Weekend is limited to 100 participants. We’ll break into smaller expert-led groups of 15 to 30 for field excursions.

Included in the price of the Science Weekend:
• All activities described in the itinerary, including a welcome evening, an expert panel discussion, two of the focused workshops or excursions, one after-hours museum soiree and a presentation by Daniel Whiteson and Jorge Cham.
• Opening reception, welcome dinner and a happy hour at the museum.
• Transportation between the base hotel and event venues.
• Recommendations for science-themed activities and sites to check out on your own before or after the
• Science Weekend, curated by Atlas Obscura and The New York Times Journeys.

Not Included:
• Breakfasts, lunches and Saturday’s dinner. (Recommendations will be provided.)
• Accommodations in Los Angeles; guests can book directly using a group discount code at the LINE Hotel, which serves as a central program venue (further details below).
• Flights or other transportation to and from Los Angeles.
• Travel insurance (recommended).

Flights, Arrival and Departure:
The weekend’s activities begin at 6 p.m. on Friday, May 10, with a welcome reception at the LINE Hotel, and end at 3:30 p.m. on Sunday, May 12. Participants coming by air should plan to arrive in Los Angeles by 3 p.m. on Friday, May 10, and to depart any time after 7:30 p.m. on Sunday, May 12. For assistance with travel arrangements, please email trips@atlasobscura.com or call (646) 961-4857.

Activity Level:
This tour is fairly active; travelers should be reasonably fit and feel comfortable walking 2 to 4 miles over the course of each day and remaining on their feet for long periods of time. Some activities involve walking on uneven ground, and up and down steps.
