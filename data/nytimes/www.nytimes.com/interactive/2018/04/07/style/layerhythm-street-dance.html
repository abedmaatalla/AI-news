    Watch These Dancers Find Spontaneity in the Circle

    By JAMES THOMAS APRIL 7, 2018

    A creative community gathers around the stage at LayeRhythm, an occasional party where random ideas turn into music and movement.

    Supported by

    By James Thomas Video and photographs by Sasha Arutyunova April 7, 2018

    LaTasha Barnes strode into the dancers’ circle, lithe and grinning and more than a little nervous, waiting to be given a theme. People in the crowd shouted suggestions; they settled on “freedom” and “Wonder Woman.”

    Behind her, the musicians noodled with interpretations while Ms. Barnes kept a relaxed shuffle, waiting for the beat to drop. Soon came an irresistible rhythm inspired by Afrobeat, rooted in house music and punctuated by stabs of keyboard that doubled as percussion. Ms. Barnes beamed and pointed at Jessie Davis, the keyboardist. She was ready to enact “freedom.”

    Ms. Barnes and Ms. Davis were featured artists at LayeRhythm, an occasional brew of partying and performing that unfolds as a series of interactive sessions in which suggestions from the crowd beget songs and steps. Imagine an improv comedy show where dancing, not laughs, is the currency. Then imagine a musicians’ jam session where the band is compelled to keep the dancers’ pace instead of the other way around.

    “We’ve lost that social connection dance used to have,” said Mai Lê Hô, who organized LayeRhythm after touring as a dancer with the revered house music producer Theo Parrish. The tour convinced her that each party is an ecosystem where everyone is related and can affect the encounter.

    “It felt empowering when she started playing,” Ms. Barnes, 37, said of how Ms. Davis’s propulsive riffs helped her explore the theme. “I just wanted to find that sense of freedom, that powerful woman inside that I want to honor every day anyway. I wanted to connect with her and make sure she knew that this was her platform.”

    “I remember really vibing with her and loving the way that she moved,” said Ms. Davis, 30, one of seven band members at January’s party — the first time she or Ms. Barnes attended. “As a band and as a unit we were flowing with the energy of the dancers.”

    LayeRhythm began in 2015 in New York and has included installments in Detroit and Philadelphia. It focuses on freestyle dancing — impromptu, unchoreographed movements driven by the spirit of the music — and is a forum for street styles like B-boying, house, waacking and flexing. D.J.s spin at the start, middle and end of the night, in periods of free mingling and dancing that play more like a conventional party.

    In New York, dance gatherings are hardly uncommon (though for decades they were illegal in some places until recent legislation) and musicians jam together frequently. But what’s distinct about LayeRhythm is the way it bridges the two groups with crowd participation.

    The event is cherished by its regulars at a time when bloated nightclub rents and the vogue of bottle service have thinned New York’s crop of dance parties, and dancing in popular culture is mostly watched, not practiced. In the last decade, local dancers have lamented the closing of venues like APT, Sullivan Room, Subrosa and Santos Party House — places that weren’t opulent but kept thoughtful and dynamic resident D.J.s, vivid sound systems and shock-absorbent flooring that forgave repeated stomps, drops and spins.

    “For many venues, the business model is rooted in alcohol sales,” said Stefan Andemicael, who was a managing partner of LayeRhythm’s original home, Meridian 23 in Chelsea. “Because they’re dancers, their body is their temple. So it’s a bit of an impasse between those venues and those kind of events. We tried to strike a balance of earning revenue and being culture-forward.”

    After Meridian 23 was bought by new owners in 2016, LayeRhythm took more than a year to find its current home at Nublu on the Lower East Side. January’s party was the first New York installment to charge a cover: $10. Its next affair this month will be a three-year anniversary celebration.

    If a night of unrehearsed movement to crowdsourced songs by a band with no sheet music sounds as if it could go terribly wrong, consider that Ms. Hô always frontloads the crowd with her acquaintances from years dancing, teaching and organizing events within New York’s robust dance culture. Some of her guests have been finalists on “So You Think You Can Dance,” performed with Madonna and danced on Broadway.

    “What you get with dancers is they have good word of mouth,” Ms. Hô said. “They’re faithful to a good party. And they bring their friends.”

    LayeRhythm performers often come from It’s Showtime NYC!, a program partly funded by the New York City Department of Cultural Affairs that gives training, professional development and performance opportunities to dancers who might otherwise illegally perform on the subway. Ms. Hô is its program director. Quinn Brown, 25, danced in subway aisles until joining It’s Showtime. At January’s LayeRhythm, he was a featured performer.

    Desmond Howard, a musician in the current stage production of “Stomp,” was featured in the session called Dance Upon a Rhyme, in which a rapper and a dancer improvise cooperatively. Spinning the term “orange” into verses about social and political strife, the rapper iLLspokinn sandwiched them in a chorus that spoke to the role of art as a coping mechanism: “But orange you glad we got music?”

    Most of those verses can’t be printed here. But over an agitating hard rock beat, Mr. Howard interpreted them faithfully, thrashing, pursing his lips and jabbing middle fingers in the air.

    “We get to know each other through movement,” said Tatiana Desardouin, 34, who has been to almost every LayeRhythm and has performed with Ms. Hô at the Apollo Theater and the Bronx Academy of Arts and Dance as part of Passion Fruit Dance Company. “The way you dance tells me how comfortable you are in your own body, how free you are moving through the world. It requires you to accept the idea that people might see into you.”

    This openness is stark in the Cupid session, which usually happens after the intermission midway through the evening. In Cupid, two musicians are randomly chosen to improvise a duet, and two dancers are picked to conjure a love story.

    “It’s like getting to know someone without talking. You just have to trust that your partner will receive what you’re gonna give,” said Gabriel Alvarez, 28. He and Kamilla Karpalovaite had already met by chance at a hustle workshop a couple of days before an audience member selected them to perform in this evening’s Cupid. But they hadn’t danced together.

    “I wouldn’t call it flirting, but it was a kind of a mix of flirting and figuring each other out. Getting comfortable in the moment,” Ms. Karpalovaite said.

    If dancing with others is an intimate act, LayeRhythm makes it communal. At every party, before the first session of the night, the host Malik Work gathers everyone in a wide semicircle around the stage and discusses world events. After Meridian 23’s closing, September’s party came 16 months, two hurricane seasons and one presidential election after the last. “Too much going on in the world. We need this,” he said.

    A recent LayeRhythm fell days after Ms. Hô’s 30th birthday. That evening the audience, backed by the house band, sang to her as dancers served everyone cake. “I consider this crowd my N.Y.C. family,” she said. “They're the same people I spend my birthday, Christmas or New Year's Eve with.”

    The penultimate session, called Rhythm Condukta, is often the most resonant, but January’s was especially meaningful. It’s reserved for someone deeply respected in the culture — an “O.G.” — and makes that dancer a whirling maestro, directing individual members of the band through movements, gestures and eye contact. There’s a sense of ceremony to it.

    This night the O.G. was Ejoe Wilson, 48, a stalwart of the house style who danced for Mariah Carey. Smirking, he opened by approaching Avishai Rozen, the drummer, and started a loose-hipped, forward-and-back salsa pattern. Mr. Rozen responded with a bubbling cadence. As Mr. Wilson cued the rest of the musicians, the song built to a soulful, infectious throb.

    When he ended his freestyle several minutes later, Mr. Wilson glistened and his chest was heaving. Then he took the microphone, sat on the edge of the stage and gave a speech about Raymond Ultarte, a dancer, promoter and mentor who appeared in videos for Jay-Z, Run-D.M.C. and Busta Rhymes. His death at 50 years old last October sent currents of grief through the dance community. He’s remembered for bringing together practitioners of different styles and generations and dutifully finding work for other artists.

    “That was for Voodoo Ray,” Mr. Wilson began. “Even after he passed away, he’s still connecting people. Look at you all.”

    The sting is still sharp. After Super Bowl LII in February, clips and screen shots of Eddie Morales, dancing alongside Justin Timberlake during the televised halftime show in a hat bearing Mr. Ultarte’s nickname, circulated through social media. So did tributes from prominent musicians and D.J.s as news of his passing spread.

    “So many people lost their best friend in October,” said Joseph Anthony Hernandez, better known as D.J. Tony Touch, a member of the seminal dance troupe Rock Steady Crew. Since 2008, the Funkbox and Toca Tuesday parties he and Mr. Ultarte hosted have been a weekly nexus for New York’s house and hip-hop dancers. Mr. Hernandez has kept the parties going in honor of his friend. “I want to continue to keep his energy alive.”

    Toward the end of the night, it was time for the final ritual known as Switch.

    Half the musicians abandoned the stage for the dance floor or grabbed another instrument. Romainson Romain, until that point a spectator, took over the drum kit and hammered out a beat. “At some point someone put a guitar in my hand,” Ms. Davis recalls. A man who’d functioned as a photographer all evening got behind the turntables and ably scratched in time to the new song.

    There was no dissonance, no lost quality in the music or the movement. The strength of Switch owes to the chemistry of individuals creating together, Ms. Desardouin said.

    "When it comes to music, you get drawn in by harmony,” she said. “Same when you dance.”

    The dancers’ circle dissolved into itself and nearly everyone who wasn’t onstage began to move to the rhythm, discrete but in tandem, including this reporter.
