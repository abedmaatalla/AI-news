    Here is a look at the power play of the Tampa Bay Lightning, who score on nearly 30 percent of their power-play chances, the best in the N.H.L.

    This is their top power-play unit — deployed in the popular 1-3-1 formation.

    The formation gives players a lot of space to operate and creates triangles where players can shoot or have at least two passing options.

    The team’s best passer, often along the right boards, is the quarterback on the play, looking for open passing lanes.

    The player opposite the passer is the best shooter. He’ll often take one-timers (quickly released shots off a pass), trying to beat the goalie, who is shifting from side to side.

    This player is in such a dangerous position to score that the four skaters on defense stay close to him, freeing up space for other shooters.

    Brayden Point uses quick one-timers, which give the goalie little time to react. Point is tied for the league lead in power-play goals.

    The net player redirects shots, tips in rebounds and moves loose pucks back out to his teammates. (Ondrej Palat, whose numbers appear low, missed more than a month with injury.)

    There is a lone defenseman on the 1-3-1, who is in a good position to pass to open teammates for a shot.

    He also has to be quick enough to keep pucks in the zone all along the blue line. Victor Hedman (2 goals, 17 assists) is often the defenseman on this unit.

    These players account for 70 percent of the Lightning’s league-leading 49 power-play goals.
